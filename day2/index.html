Types of Hacker Attacks — Part 1: The Malware Family

"Malware" is the umbrella. Underneath, each type behaves differently — that's what makes them hard to defend against uniformly.

🦠

Virus

Attaches to a file. Needs you to run the file to spread. Like a parasite needing a host.

🐛

Worm

Self-replicating. Spreads across a network without human help. WannaCry, Stuxnet, Morris worm.

🐴

Trojan

Disguises as legitimate software. The user installs it willingly. The damage runs silently in the background.

🔒

Ransomware

Encrypts your files. Demands payment (usually crypto) for the key. Often spreads as a worm too.

👁️

Spyware

Watches everything: keystrokes, screenshots, cameras. Pegasus is the most famous example.

💰

Adware / PUPs

Annoying ads, browser hijackers. Often bundled with "free" software.

2:35 – 3:00

Part 2: Phishing, Social Engineering, & Password Attacks

Why hack the system when you can hack the human? 90%+ of breaches start with one tired person clicking one wrong link.

📧 Lab 2 — Phishing Email Inspector SPOT THE RED FLAGS

Below are three real-looking phishing emails. Click the highlighted parts to reveal what's wrong. How many red flags can you find?

From: PayPal Security <security@paypal-verify.support> ⚠ paypal-verify.support is NOT paypal.com
To: you@example.com · Today, 3:42 AM
⚠ URGENT: Your account will be suspended in 24 hours

Dear Valued Customer,

We detected unsual activity on your account. To prevent suspension, please verify your identity here within 24 hours.

Failure to act will result in permanent account closure and forfeiture of your balance.

Thank you,
PayPal Security Team

🚩 The sender domain is paypal-verify.support — NOT paypal.com. Real PayPal emails always come from @paypal.com. The hyphen-and-fake-TLD trick is classic.
🚩 The "verify" link actually points to http://paypa1-verify.ru/login — that's a "1" instead of "l", and a Russian TLD. Always hover before clicking.
Other red flags to spot: urgency ("24 hours"), typo ("unsual"), vague greeting ("Valued Customer"), threat ("forfeiture").
From: Rajesh Kumar, CEO <r.kumar.ceo@gmail.com> ⚠ Gmail account — your CEO has a corporate email!
To: you@company.com · Today, 11:55 AM
Quick favour — need this done in next 30 min

Hi,

I'm in a meeting and can't talk. Need you to buy 10 × ₹2,000 Amazon gift cards for a client appreciation gesture. Send me the codes ASAP. I'll reimburse later today.

Don't loop in anyone else — this is confidential.

Rajesh

🚩 Your real CEO doesn't email from @gmail.com. This is "CEO fraud" / Business Email Compromise (BEC) — the FBI says it's caused $50B+ in losses since 2013.
Other flags: urgency ("30 min"), gift cards (untraceable!), secrecy ("don't loop in anyone"), authority pressure. Always verify out-of-band — call the person.
From: Microsoft 365 <noreply@microsft-online.com> ⚠ "microsft" — missing 'o'. Typosquat.
To: you@company.com · Yesterday, 7:18 PM
You have (3) new voicemails waiting

You have 3 new voice messages in your Microsoft 365 mailbox.

🎧 Click here to listen to your voicemails

This message will be deleted in 7 days.

The Microsoft 365 Team

🚩 Domain is microsft-online.com — missing the "o" in "microsoft". Classic typosquatting. Microsoft uses microsoft.com / outlook.com / live.com.
🚩 The link goes to a fake login page that harvests your Microsoft credentials, then redirects you to the real Microsoft login so you never notice. This is called credential harvesting.

3:25 – 3:45

Hacktivism: Politics with Packets

Hacktivism = hacking + activism. Using cyber-attacks to make a political or social statement. Where do you draw the ethical line?

🎭

Anonymous

Decentralised collective. Operations against Scientology, ISIS, Russia, Iran. Famous Guy Fawkes mask.

💧

WikiLeaks

Publishing leaked documents. Iraq War logs, Cablegate, Vault 7. Founder Julian Assange — controversial.

😈

LulzSec

"For the lulz." 50-day rampage in 2011 — Sony, PBS, Senate, CIA. Most members jailed.

🇮🇳

Indian Cyber Force

Defacement & DDoS against perceived national adversaries. Counter-groups exist too.

⚖️ Lab 9 — Where's the Ethical Line? CASE STUDIES

5 real hacktivism events. You decide: was it justified? Then see how legal/ethical scholars actually view it. There's no single right answer.

The hard truth: Even attacks against "bad" targets are illegal in most jurisdictions. Indian IT Act Section 66 carries up to 3 years in jail for unauthorised access — regardless of motive. Ethical disclosure (bug bounty, CERT-In reporting) achieves the same accountability goals legally.
