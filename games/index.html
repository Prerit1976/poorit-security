Game 1

🕵️ Password Hunt — OSINT Challenge

Meet Priya. She uses passwords tied to her life — like most people. Read her story carefully, then guess her 5 passwords using the hints. This is exactly how real attackers profile targets.

Priya Sharma woke at 6:30 AM in her Bengaluru apartment, where she had lived since moving from Pune in 2019. Today was her 29th birthday — September 14, 1996 was etched into every official form she had ever filled. She made her morning filter coffee while her tabby cat, Mango, demanded breakfast with a chorus of complaints. Mango had been her constant companion for exactly 7 years, ever since she adopted him from a shelter in 2018.

As a marketing manager at a fintech startup called CashFlow, Priya was constantly on her phone. Her preferred password manager held over 200 accounts — but she still had a few "easy" ones she remembered manually. The one for her Netflix account, which she shared with her partner Karan, was based on their favourite restaurant in Bengaluru: a small dosa place called Vidyarthi Bhavan. Their first date there in 2021 was a treasured memory.

Her car, a 2023 silver Maruti Swift, was nicknamed "Bullet" by her dad — a joke since it was anything but fast. The car's number plate was KA-03-MH-7842, and she used the last four digits as the PIN for almost everything: her debit card, her gym locker, even her phone unlock code. Her dad always teased her: "Priya, that's too easy to guess!"

She had been to twelve different countries — her travel goal was 30 by age 35. Her favourite was Japan, which she visited in 2022 and tattooed a small cherry blossom on her wrist to commemorate. On Instagram, where she had 12,400 followers, her handle was @priyas_lens — combining her name with her love of photography.

Yesterday, she received an email warning that her accounts may be compromised. Today, she would find out why.

1 Netflix password

💡 Based on the restaurant where Priya and Karan had their first date. Just the name, lowercase, no spaces.

2 Debit card PIN

💡 She uses the same 4-digit PIN for everything — it's the last 4 digits of her car number plate.

3 Banking app password (DDMMYYYY)

💡 Her date of birth in DDMMYYYY format (no separators).

4 Email password

💡 Her pet's name + her pet's age. Joined together, lowercase. (Hint: paragraph 1 has both facts.)

5 Instagram password (she reused the handle!)

💡 People often reuse their username as a password. Her Instagram handle (without the @ symbol).

🏆 All 5 cracked!

This is exactly what attackers do — gather public info, then guess passwords. The lesson: never base passwords on anything publicly knowable about you. Use a password manager with random strings.

How attackers actually do this: A 4-digit PIN has only 10,000 possibilities. A computer tries all of them in under a second. The only thing protecting your debit card is rate limiting — the bank blocks the card after 3 wrong guesses. Without that, brute-force is trivial.
